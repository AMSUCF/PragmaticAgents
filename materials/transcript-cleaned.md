I find myself thinking about a visual metaphor based on Math Blaster. I've selected this because I'm going to draw a line between the early days of edutainment that I grew up on and the current use of chatbots. Math Blaster did not, in fact, make me love math. I was captain of the math team at one point, but that's neither here nor there. It did make me a gamer. And the chatbot interface and the ways that chatbots, especially as a generalist educational tool that's supposed to push education forward, remind me of many well-intentioned experiments in edutainment—or what Brenda Laurel memorably called "chocolate-covered broccoli" (*Utopian Entrepreneur*, 2001). But in this case, it's a bit more insidious, because Math Blaster was a really well-designed interactive worksheet as a game. That was kind of the low bar for what game-based education could be—what we call gamification rather than meaningful play or serious game design. There was no actual correlation between content, the math, and these game mechanics. And similarly, the chatbot is being deployed as this one-size-fits-all educational tool, often with the intention of making something easier or more accessible, and it's most likely just going to reduce it. The chatbot actually reduces friction to an outcome and in doing so encourages reductive production.

And here I want to invoke a definition that circulated on Bluesky: AI slop is something that requires less effort to produce than it does to consume (see also Furze, "The Effort Economy of Slop"). And this is why so many of us are frustrated with the existence of chatbots and the idea of having a chatbot write an essay. But I want to kind of contend here that it's possible to use AI in a way that is in fact labor-intensive, that requires expertise and knowledge, and that produces weird, interesting, compelling, and experimental results. But a chatbot is not going to get you there, because the chatbot interface and what we're offering our students in Copilot right now isn't designed for sustained intentionality. It's designed for rapid results and quick engagement, not unlike gamification or early edutainment. It is designed to keep you on the system, even if the outcomes are not that great.

So the difference between chatbots and agentic tools is that agentic tools also require expertise. They reward and extend expertise. I don't really want to get all McLuhan on you, but there is something about how agentic tools work as an extension of ourselves (McLuhan, *Understanding Media*, 1964) that's very different from what a chatbot offers. And when we're bringing AI into the classroom, the fact that we're bringing a technology that offers quick, easy replacement and succinct solutions—the illusion of progress—instead of tools that require intentionality, thoughtful design, and that can lead to meaningful, creative, expressive, and research outputs, it's just thoroughly depressing.

When I look at what's red-listed on the "AI for All" lists, I would almost reverse it. If I were in control of this system, using a chatbot like Grok, for instance—currently on the yellow list, I believe—is more likely to lead you to glib responses and reductiveness, particularly given the political nature of the platform. Meanwhile, the tool I use all the time, that I'm going to be primarily demoing today, Claude Code, does not even appear on UCF's list—an interesting omission given that it is, at least according to some of the conversations between faculty, staff, and the CIO, a highly in-demand tool at UCF.

Claude Cowork, meanwhile, offers a really interesting middle ground in how it obscures certain aspects of the work while enabling others. I'll show you that as well. But both Cowork and Code and other agentic tools demand that you provide significant context. They thrive on knowledge input, guidance, and management. Here I want to invoke Minas Karamanis's recent essay "The Machines Are Fine. I'm Worried About Us" (Karamanis, *ergosphere.blog*, 2026), which draws a sharp distinction between an expert building a research project with Claude Code and a grad student using it as a shortcut—his Alice and Bob parable, where the paper looks identical but the scientist doesn't. The point is to say that agentic outputs genuinely reflect expertise, whereas chatbot outputs reflect training data and a little bit of literacy regarding how one prompts the system and what system one chooses. There's very little literacy to build with a chatbot, except perhaps recognizing how easily manipulated we are by them—per the recent study in *Science* on the political persuasion capacity of conversational AI (Bai et al., "The Levers of Political Persuasion with Conversational Artificial Intelligence," *Science*, 2025), and the study on cognitive offloading that was going around Bluesky (Gerlich, "AI Tools in Society: Impacts on Cognitive Offloading and the Future of Critical Thinking," *Societies* 15.1, 2025), which is being exaggerated a bit, but again, really speaks to the chatbot interface.

Agentic tools, by contrast, have mostly been designed for software developers and for computer scientists. They can continue the pattern of how I've worked with AI forever, which is to say they're intimidating, and they're being resisted by IT—which Ethan Mollick writes about as one of the biggest problems we have right now with AI: that our AI usage is being decided by a department that's worried about risk aversion (Mollick, "The IT Department: Where AI Goes to Die," *One Useful Thing*). And we're talking about weird tools with serious potential to augment creativity and research that cannot be so easily regulated or defined.

There are awesome examples of weird AI, like the digital humanities project I want to highlight here: Mr. Chatterbox, a language model trained entirely from scratch on over 28,000 Victorian-era British texts from the British Library (Venturella, "Mr. Chatterbox," 2026; see also Willison's discussion at simonwillison.net)—with this fantastic process reflection. And that's an example of using Claude Code as a tool to build something, which is one of the things I'm going to show you today. The resulting output is not Claude. It's not a frontier model. It's not Anthropic's. It can be brought into a classroom without any data risks. In fact, it's also the sort of thing that our students can build for themselves. Wouldn't we rather have students who are makers and creators than passive consumers of a chatbot's output? So much of what text-based chat or even generative image generation does is cultural flattening. But it doesn't have to be. We have an opportunity to push for something better.

---

Ethan Mollick has talked about the problem that campuses like ours are facing (Mollick, "Management as AI Superpower," *One Useful Thing*), which is that traditionally software tools have been headed and distributed through enterprise licenses and overseen by IT—and IT is typically a department whose job is in part to handle risk. And everything I've just shown you has risks. Creative code and playing with software through this type of agency is a risky endeavor, and that risky endeavor is what makes it so difficult to just categorize in terms of productivity gains, or to even see its impact on the workforce in any places outside of traditional software development.

So all of these things are usages that are evolving. I spend a lot of my time paying attention to how people are experimenting, and the people who are experimenting are not working within the constraints of an institution like this. We are working outside of it.

So why do I want to encourage you to join the ranks of experimenters, to take on some risk, perhaps on your own devices or even on your own phone? Because what people are experimenting with now is how fundamentally computational interfaces, software, and how we work is moving. Some of this is going to be standardized, and as it's standardized, it's going to be less playful, less open to your creativity. If you hate chatbots the way I hate chatbots, you know just how very rigid they can be, and how often you pull the slot machine that is those devices and don't actually get what you were looking for.

So agentic tools give you way more creative control. They allow you to build processes where you know exactly where the data and decisions are coming from, or you can take a lot of control over those pieces.

And it may be that UCF, as a large institution, can't enable that type of play anytime soon. There are some really exciting changes in how this all works that also point to what is going to be possible.

And here I want to point to that recent essay on how LLMs work (see Mitchell, "On Evaluating Cognitive Capabilities in Machines," *AI: A Guide for Thinking Humans*, 2026). Now, you've heard the "stochastic parrots" (Bender et al., "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots," *FAccT*, 2021) and all of the "AI is just a synthesized text machine." Okay, but that is no longer true. There are three components to what's happening when we work with an agentic tool right now. The lowest level is the actual LLM, and these models themselves are certainly getting better in terms of the amount of data and how they're trained—we could go down that rabbit hole, but we're not going to. Then there's the reasoning layer on top of it, and the reasoning models, which are more expensive to run. The frontier models have an enormous amount of training on top of that, so they are not just predictive text machines by any stretch of the imagination. Then the next level is the actual harness. When I talk about Claude Code, Claude Code is a harness, and I can use Claude Code with models that are not Claude. For instance, I'm going to show this example of Claude Code using the Superpowers workflow (Vincent, "Superpowers: How I'm Using Coding Agents," 2025) with Gemma 4, a model running entirely on my desktop, so everything you're seeing here is contained. I am not using some expensive cloud computing to pull this off.

But this type of workflow, this kind of way of working with harnesses and local models, is starting to be more and more viable for different types of tasks. And that means that when people talk about AI as if it's all giant data centers and big companies and a bubble that's going to burst, I'm sorry, but they're just plain wrong. Because these harnesses and local models would still exist if the rest of it shut down tomorrow, and software and computational workflows would still never be the same again. It's also not going to shut down tomorrow. It's going to keep evolving, and the companies are actually just as interested in efficiency in many cases as those advocating for more efficient AI tools—because simply put, they're currently subsidizing big reasoning models through things like Anthropic's giant tools with Claude Opus and Claude Code at a level that is unsustainable. And that's okay, because as they stop subsidizing it, the way people work with these tools will change. Local models and smaller tools for tasks that don't require the big intensive workhorses of frontier models will be more normalized and more available, and people who are working on really complicated problems will continue to benefit from the fact that these are, in a way, an approach to work that is difficult to exhaust. They are persistent, and that persistence really shows through in areas like cybersecurity, but it can also benefit us in domains like the construction and maintenance of archives we have in the arts and humanities.

The ability to use this as a cultural and computational interface—and in doing so, to make up for some of the drops in funding and the drops in what we can in fact support in labor. Using agentic tools to support our work. If you've ever had things you really wanted to build that, frankly, you don't have the time or resources to build—those rejected grants, those grants that are going nowhere in today's climate at institutions—these are ways to build the things you're dreaming about. And for me, it's become a way to unlock all those side projects and side quests that I had, frankly, almost given up on.
